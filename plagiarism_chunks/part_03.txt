The project explicitly discards outdated, percentage-based macro splits (e.g., the standard 40/40/20 split) in favor of evidence-based protein targeting (prescribing exactly 1.6 to 1.8 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight for muscle synthesis). For energy calculations, Elevate utilizes the scientifically validated Mifflin-St Jeor equation, but advances it by applying dynamic, algorithmically calculated intensity multipliers derived from the user's daily Volume Load (Sets × Reps × Weight). Technical and Architectural Background: From a software engineering perspective, building a system capable of real-time pose tracking, machine learning inference, and secure user management within a single monolithic codebase is highly inefficient. Therefore, the background of this project is deeply rooted in Microservices Architecture. The system utilizes Node.js for its non-blocking I/O model, making it the perfect lightweight gateway for handling hundreds of concurrent user authentication requests and database reads. Conversely, Python was selected for the secondary backend due to its unparalleled ecosystem for data science and artificial intelligence. The integration of XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting) a highly efficient implementation of gradient boosted decision trees provides the background for the system's multi-target predictive modeling. Unlike deep learning networks which require massive datasets and obscure their decision-making logic in a "black box," XGBoost was chosen for its rapid execution and high interpretability when analyzing tabular biometric data. Finally, the integration of Computer Vision frameworks allows the software to perform complex skeletal mapping directly from standard 2D webcam feeds, removing the need for specialized motion-capture hardware. By synthesizing these advanced computational backgrounds with rigorously tested physiological theories, Elevate transcends traditional fitness tracking to become a fully adaptive, scientifically backed health management platform. CHAPTER 3: PROBLEM DEFINITION AND SCOPE 3.1 Problem Definition The current ecosystem of digital fitness and health applications suffers from a severe lack of adaptability, integration, and algorithmic intelligence. While wearable technology and mobile applications have successfully mastered the art of data collection, they critically fail at data prescription. The core problem this project addresses is the "one-size-fits-all" methodology that dominates health technology. Specifically, the overarching problem can be broken down into four major technical and physiological flaws present in existing software architecture: The Failure of Static Template Assignment: The vast majority of traditional fitness applications operate as simple query-based databases. When a user creates an account, they are mapped to a pre-written fitness template based on incredibly broad categories (e.g., categorizing a 20-year-old college student and a 50-year-old office worker both as "Beginner Weightloss"). This ignores individual body metrics, specific equipment constraints, and precise biological entropy. Because the software does not algorithmically generate unique routines, users plateau quickly, lose motivation, and experience high abandonment rates. The Disconnect Between Nutrition and Physical Exertion: Existing systems treat dietary tracking and exercise programming as entirely isolated silos. A user typically calculates their Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) once, and the application instructs them to consume the exact same macronutrient split (e.g., 2000 calories, 150g protein, 200g carbs) every single day. This is physiologically flawed. A training day involving intense compound movements (such as barbell squats and deadlifts) creates significantly more metabolic stress, central nervous system fatigue, and muscle damage than a sedentary rest day or a light cardio session. Because traditional software fails to dynamically measure the specific "Volume Load" of a workout, it cannot adjust the user's nutritional targets to match their daily intensity, leading to suboptimal recovery and poor muscle synthesis. The Misinterpretation and Mismanagement of Raw Biometric Data: Fitness applications frequently encourage users to log raw biometric data, such as the exact hours they slept or the liters of water they consumed. However, raw data without medical context is algorithmically useless. If an algorithm attempts to use raw numbers (like "6.5 hours of sleep") directly against a standardized 1-10 fatigue scale, the mathematical logic breaks down. This results in the system calculating inaccurate "Readiness" or "Recovery" scores. Consequently, the application might dangerously prescribe a high-intensity interval training (HIIT) session to a user who is severely sleep-deprived and dehydrated, simply because the software could not translate raw variables into normalized health metrics. The Lack of Accessible Biomechanical Feedback: Incorrect exercise execution is the leading cause of gym-related musculoskeletal injuries. While human personal trainers can immediately correct a client's form in real-time, digital applications only provide static video demonstrations. Users exercising at home or in isolation lack an automated, accessible system to spot and correct their skeletal posture in real-time. Without external feedback, users repeatedly perform movements with dangerous biomechanics, risking severe injury. 3.2 Scope The scope of Elevate encompasses the complete end-to-end development of a fully integrated, full-stack, AI-powered fitness coaching application. The system is designed using a microservices architecture to handle both lightweight state management and heavy data-science computations. This ensures that no two users ever receive the exact same routine, and that the routine remains stable across logins unless the user updates their physical profile.
